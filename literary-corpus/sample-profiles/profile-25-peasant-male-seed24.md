> [!background] Background
> **Age 18 (696 TR)** — A dog followed him home one evening and refused to leave. He let it stay.
> **Age 19 (697 TR)** — He was summoned with the other men of the township to view a body and give verdict to the coroner. The face of the dead stayed with him.
> **Age 20 (698 TR)** — The hundred moot is held monthly and most months he has no business there. This month he does — a minor dispute about a field boundary that has been running since before he inherited the holding. He has the documentation. The other party also has documentation. The moot will look at both sets of documentation and reach a finding. He is not certain the finding will favour him. He has prepared an appeal to the shire assize on the assumption it won't, which is not pessimism but preparation. The moot is a public proceeding and everyone in the room knows everyone else and their business. This is simultaneously the moot's strength and its limitation.

> **Age 29 (707 TR)** — The royal forester caught him with a deer on royal forest land. He is a first offender, which is the only good part of this. First offenders are beaten — formally, by the forester's men, which is different from being beaten informally in that it is recorded and witnessed and the number of strokes is specified and he can stand in front of anyone afterward and say it was done correctly under the law. He accepted it without resistance because resistance is how a first offence becomes a hanging offence. He is not going back to the royal forest. The deer are not worth it now that he knows exactly what the foresters are willing to do to a man they catch alone in the wood.

> **Age 35 (713 TR)** — The knight stopped him on the road and asked about the sword. It was a shortsword — a simple weapon by every classification in *Manners and Affectations* — and he was a freeman, and carrying a simple weapon is his right. He stated his status clearly and named the section of the arms classification that applied. The knight looked at the sword, looked at him, decided it wasn't worth the argument, and rode on. He stood in the road for a moment afterward noting what he had felt during the exchange, which was primarily a very sharp awareness that the knight's decision could have gone the other way and that the law's protection depends on the armed man deciding to respect it.
