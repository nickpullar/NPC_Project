> [!background] Background
> **Age 18 (686 TR)** — He left in November, which is the worst time to travel and the best time to leave unnoticed — the roads are empty and the lord's reeve does not expect movement. He reached the chartered town in four days and presented himself to the town officer and declared his intent to remain. The year and a day is not a comfortable year. It is a year of knowing that anyone from the home village who recognises you and reports to your lord can end it. He did the work available to him, he stayed within the town boundaries, he did not draw attention. At the end of the year and a day the town officer acknowledged him a freeman. He has never returned to the village. He sends money when he can.
> **Age 19 (687 TR)** — The lord's steward reclassified him as a serf when the estate changed hands. He disputed it. The dispute went to the hundred moot, which is one of the few places where a man can contest whether he is free, because the question of whether he is free cannot be decided by the lord's court — the lord's court does not have jurisdiction over a man who may not be in the lord's jurisdiction. He obtained the writ himself, which cost six pence and three days of lost work. The moot found him a freeholder. The steward accepted the finding with an expression that suggested he considered it temporary. He has kept the moot record.
> **Age 20 (688 TR)** — The crop rotted or the rains came late. Winter would be lean.
> **Age 21 (689 TR)** — Brigands relieved him of coin and dignity alike.
> **Age 22 (690 TR)** — The grain came in heavy. The lord's share still left enough.
> **Age 23 (691 TR)** — He almost missed it — a seam in the floor, a weight in the soil that wasn't right. What he found there changed his year, and perhaps more than his year.
> **Age 24 (692 TR)** — The fever lasted three weeks. He spent the fourth week deciding what the experience had changed, if anything. The illness passed without lasting harm.
> **Age 25 (693 TR)** — He rode out whenever duty permitted. The forest taught him patience and observation.
> **Age 26 (694 TR)** — Crisis drew him back to his faith with new fervour.
> **Age 27 (695 TR)** — Someone he knew — not a close friend, but a familiar face from the guild, the village, the neighbourhood — was killed. The world felt less safe after that.
> **Age 28 (696 TR)** — Another difficult year. Life continued.
> **Age 29 (697 TR)** — The Serolan heard both parties in the chapter house. He was fair in the sense that neither of them got what they wanted. The settlement was binding by custom if not by law and cost him less than a court would have and rather more than he thought the matter was worth.
> **Age 30 (698 TR)** — The illness had stripped him down. He spent months rebuilding, finding it slower than he had expected.
> **Age 31 (699 TR)** — Crisis drew him back to his faith with new fervour.
> **Age 32 (700 TR)** — He saw something that was probably his business and kept walking. He thought about it for several weeks.
> **Age 33 (701 TR)** — He made a throw — of a stone, a knife, a horseshoe — that was perfect and that he was never able to repeat. There were witnesses.
> **Age 34 (702 TR)** — The knight stopped him on the road and asked about the sword. It was a shortsword — a simple weapon by every classification in *Manners and Affectations* — and he was a freeman, and carrying a simple weapon is his right. He stated his status clearly and named the section of the arms classification that applied. The knight looked at the sword, looked at him, decided it wasn't worth the argument, and rode on. He stood in the road for a moment afterward noting what he had felt during the exchange, which was primarily a very sharp awareness that the knight's decision could have gone the other way and that the law's protection depends on the armed man deciding to respect it.
> **Age 35 (703 TR)** — The wound had gone bad by the time they brought him to the hospital sisters. He did not remember most of the following week. What he remembered afterward was the smell of herbs and the sound of someone — it might have been the same woman each time — sitting with him in the dark hours and not speaking. He recovered. He did not take this for granted.
> **Age 36 (704 TR)** — Crisis drew him back to his faith with new fervour.
> **Age 37 (705 TR)** — The shout went up from the market quarter and he was close enough that joining was not optional — the custom is that any man who hears it and does not join is liable to be treated as an accomplice. He ran with the others for three streets before they cornered the man in a yard. The man had stolen a purse. He held one arm while the officers bound him. The whole thing lasted perhaps ten minutes. He thought about it afterward: the specific way the crowd becomes a legal instrument when the right words are shouted, and how different it felt to be part of that instrument than he had expected it to feel.
> **Age 38 (706 TR)** — An accident or fight left him with a wound that did not heal cleanly. A grievous wound to the head or face. The damage was permanent — hearing reduced by 3.
> **Age 39 (707 TR)** — The sky held patterns any farmer learned to read. He had finally learned to read them.
> **Age 41 (709 TR)** — He endured. The details were different; the shape was the same.
> **Age 42 (710 TR)** — He made a habit of it — the early mornings, the regular exertion. It accumulated.
> **Age 43 (711 TR)** — He was certain of something and wrong. He argued his position at length before the evidence became impossible to ignore.
> **Age 44 (712 TR)** — He hung the washing out. It rained within the hour. He took it in again. He did not mention this to anyone.
> **Age 45 (713 TR)** — Another difficult year. Life continued.
> **Age 46 (714 TR)** — Time had softened what once seemed unforgivable. They made their peace. Reconciled with Selyn Idsen
> **Age 47 (715 TR)** — The lord held court in the hall and he was summoned as a party to a dispute about labour obligations. The lord sat in the chair at the end of the hall and heard both sides in the specific way that lords hear disputes: with one eye on the merits and one eye on whether the outcome will produce two functional tenants or one resentful one. The finding went against him on the main point and in his favour on the secondary point, which is a finding designed to ensure that both parties leave with something. He accepted it. The lord's court is not impartial — the lord is also his landlord — but it is not arbitrary either. It is interested, which is a different thing.
> **Age 48 (716 TR)** — Something he planted as a throwaway grew into something worth having. He took more care the following year.
> **Age 49 (717 TR)** — Another difficult year. Life continued.
> **Age 50 (718 TR)** — He reached out, or they did. The thing between them was old enough to look smaller than it had. Reconciled with Selyn Idsen
> **Age 51 (719 TR)** — He sat alone watching a fire go down until there was nothing left but ash. He had not intended to sit that long.
> **Age 52 (720 TR)** — It had begun as professional acquaintance. Somewhere in the course of years it became something else — not sentimentally, but in the way that trust accumulates. Selyn Idsen became a close friend
