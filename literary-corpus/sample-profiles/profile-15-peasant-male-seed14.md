> [!background] Background
> **Age 18 (706 TR)** — The crop rotted or the rains came late. Winter would be lean.
> **Age 19 (707 TR)** — He married. The arrangement made sense practically; whether it would make sense in other ways remained to be seen. Married Alura Halsen (peasant, age 17)
> **Age 19 (707 TR)** — His wife lost the child before its time. He did not know what to do with his grief. week 15
> **Age 20 (708 TR)** — The lord's steward reclassified him as a serf when the estate changed hands. He disputed it. The dispute went to the hundred moot, which is one of the few places where a man can contest whether he is free, because the question of whether he is free cannot be decided by the lord's court — the lord's court does not have jurisdiction over a man who may not be in the lord's jurisdiction. He obtained the writ himself, which cost six pence and three days of lost work. The moot found him a freeholder. The steward accepted the finding with an expression that suggested he considered it temporary. He has kept the moot record.
> **Age 20 (708 TR)** — The child came still. They had felt it move. He had no words for what followed. week 32
> **Age 21 (709 TR)** — The reeve came round in the autumn as he always does, checking the strip yields against the assessment and collecting the lord's share. The assessment this year was wrong — not maliciously wrong, the reeve was not that kind of man, but wrong in the way things are wrong when the person making the assessment has fifteen holdings to assess and not enough days to do it properly. He disputed it at the village meeting. The reeve revised two items. The third item they disagreed about and the reeve's assessment stood. He accepted this. The reeve's assessment standing is not the same as the reeve being right. It means the lord agrees with the reeve, which is a different thing. He will dispute it again next autumn.
> **Age 21 (709 TR)** — He watched his father — or mother — work through something difficult without complaining. He understood something that he could not have put into words.
> **Age 22 (710 TR)** — Two years underground made him a freeman. The guild gave him a tin medallion as legal proof, which he wore for a decade before it was stolen and which he has spent twenty years wishing he still had. His former lord sent someone to contest the freedom in the first year. The mine moot ruled for him. He did not return to his home village. He has not been back in thirty years and thinks about it occasionally.
> **Age 23 (711 TR)** — The franchise was available — a small workshop in the wool quarter, nothing spectacular but solid — and he turned it down. He told the guild officer he wanted another two years of journeyman work first. The guild officer wrote this down without visible opinion. The other journeymen had opinions, most of them unflattering. He is aware that he is making a decision most people in his position do not make and he is making it for reasons he cannot fully articulate, which is perhaps the real reason: he is not yet sure he is as good as he needs to be, and he would rather know before he has a workshop depending on him.
> **Age 24 (712 TR)** — He was summoned with the other men of the township to view a body and give verdict to the coroner. The face of the dead stayed with him.
> **Age 24 (712 TR)** — His spouse produced a meal so badly wrong that it became a story they told for decades. He ate it without comment.
> **Age 25 (713 TR)** — The piece is done. He has done everything he knows how to do to it. Now it sits on the bench and he waits for the master to look at it and say something, which the master has not yet done because the master is finishing something else. This is a large fraction of an apprentice's life: doing the work, then waiting to find out what the work meant. He is getting better at the waiting. He uses the time to look at the piece from different angles and find the things the master will find before the master finds them.
> **Age 25 (713 TR)** — He and his spouse had an argument about something so minor he could not remember it two weeks later. The atmosphere lasted much longer.
> **Age 26 (714 TR)** — The disaster came without warning. What had taken years to build was gone in hours.
> **Age 26 (714 TR)** — The pregnancy ended before it had properly begun. He did not know what to say, so he said nothing useful. week 9
> **Age 27 (715 TR)** — The court found him guilty. What followed was swift and public.
> **Age 27 (715 TR)** — He held the infant and understood something new about the world. He had not expected to feel it so immediately. week 40 Yara Coring born (female)
> **Age 28 (716 TR)** — The illness had stripped him down. He spent months rebuilding, finding it slower than he had expected.
> **Age 28 (716 TR)** — He endured. The details were different; the shape was the same.
> **Age 29 (717 TR)** — He made something — a wooden figure, a knotted rope, a small carving — with no purpose in mind. He gave it away.
> **Age 29 (717 TR)** — His wife fell ill — not the brief fever of a week, but something that lingered and frightened. She recovered, mostly. He did not forget the months of it.
> **Age 30 (718 TR)** — Something happened — he could never explain what to outsiders — that had the whole household laughing until they couldn't stand. He still smiled at the memory years later.
> **Age 31 (719 TR)** — Crisis drew him back to his faith with new fervour.
> **Age 32 (720 TR)** — There is a journeyman three years ahead of him who considers the apprentices his personal labour reserve. This is not unusual — the structure permits it — but the specific use he makes of it has crossed into something the master would probably not endorse if he were paying attention. He has made the calculation: confronting the journeyman directly produces a worse outcome than working within the structure while quietly ensuring the master sees the results of the arrangement. He is learning that craft skill and workplace politics are not the same skill and that both are necessary.
> **Age 32 (720 TR)** — The child lived, but it was plain from the first days that something was different. He loved the child completely and did not know what the future held. week 40 — lame Dalla Coring born (female, lame)
> **Age 32 (720 TR)** — Dalla Coring died — the lame child he had worried over for years. Dalla Coring died (age 0)
