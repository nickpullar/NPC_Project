> **Age 21 (697 TR)** — The lord held court in the hall and he was summoned as a party to a dispute about labour obligations. The lord sat in the chair at the end of the hall and heard both sides in the specific way that lords hear disputes: with one eye on the merits and one eye on whether the outcome will produce two functional tenants or one resentful one. The finding went against him on the main point and in his favour on the secondary point, which is a finding designed to ensure that both parties leave with something. He accepted it. The lord's court is not impartial — the lord is also his landlord — but it is not arbitrary either. It is interested, which is a different thing.

The payment took everything he had saved in the previous two years. He is now the effective head of the family holding and has begun saving again. The death tax on a freeman's estate is not technically death — it is the lord's way of reminding you that the arrangement is contractual and the contract has terms.

He obtained the writ himself, which cost six pence and three days of lost work. The moot found him a freeholder. The steward accepted the finding with an expression that suggested he considered it temporary. He has kept the moot record.
