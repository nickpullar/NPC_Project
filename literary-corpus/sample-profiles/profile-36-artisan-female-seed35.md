> [!background] Background
> **Age 18 (685 TR)** — The master put her hands on the material and showed her how the grain ran under the surface — not visually, by feel. She had been reading grain by eye. Reading it by touch produces different information. The two readings together produce a third kind of knowledge that she did not have before. She has been running her hands over every piece of material she touches since that afternoon, building the vocabulary.
> **Age 19 (686 TR)** — The commission she submitted was assessed as journeyman quality by two of the three guild examiners. The third examiner noted it was produced by a woman and recommended she be assessed against the women's track, which carries different certification than the main guild track and does not lead to a franchise. She asked for a written explanation of which guild statute required this assessment. No written explanation was forthcoming. She submitted a formal complaint to the guild registrar. The complaint is pending, which means it will remain pending until she stops caring or leaves the jurisdiction. She has not decided which she will do.
> **Age 20 (687 TR)** — The mistress cuffed her across the back of the head hard enough to make her ears ring. The reason was a miscounted measure that had ruined two yards of good cloth. She did not cry — she had learned by this point that crying produced worse outcomes than silence. She fixed the count, restarted the section, and did not make the same error again. She has thought about the incident since mostly in terms of what the mistress actually communicated: that the cloth mattered more than the learning, and that the learning was her problem to solve, not the mistress's problem to manage.
> **Age 22 (689 TR)** — Brigands relieved her of coin and dignity alike.
> **Age 23 (690 TR)** — She took her loom to her mother's house and worked from there for a year. The guild does not certify home-weavers; this is an area where the guild's coverage is incomplete by design, because the women who weave at home are not the women the guild is designed to serve. She is not sure whether this is convenient or an insult. She sold everything she produced and used the money to buy better materials. Then she went back to the guild workshop.
> **Age 24 (691 TR)** — She found someone willing to teach and gave that person her full attention. Studied under Morin Dyer
> **Age 25 (692 TR)** — The journey required more of her than she had expected and gave back more than she had hoped.
> **Age 26 (693 TR)** — The year passed much as the last had.
> **Age 27 (694 TR)** — She followed a cat through somewhere she did not know and found something she was glad to know was there.
> **Age 28 (695 TR)** — She is the only woman in the workshop. This is a fact with specific texture: not hostile, exactly, but requiring a level of navigation that the male apprentices do not perform. She has worked out what is expected of her in addition to the craft itself — how much social work is required, where the lines are, which of the journeymen are worth the investment and which are looking for a reason to be difficult. She does the work. She also does the extra work. She does not complain about this. Complaining would be a third kind of work.
> **Age 29 (696 TR)** — He endured. The details were different; the shape was the same.
> **Age 30 (697 TR)** — She lost a small thing she had carried for years. She did not speak of it but felt its absence for a long time.
> **Age 31 (698 TR)** — She ruined two yards of figured cloth that the master had been saving for a specific commission. The sound the mistress made when she saw it was not a word. She sat with that sound for a long time afterward — not out of guilt exactly, but out of the specific determination to understand precisely what had gone wrong at every stage of the decision and execution so that she could guarantee she never made those specific errors again. She can still describe all six errors in sequence. She will never make any of them.
> **Age 32 (699 TR)** — The morning begins with whatever the master left unfinished, which is the rule at every workshop and the reason early mornings in every workshop smell the same. She has been doing this for four years. She knows the rhythm of the place — which tasks come in sequence, which the master trusts her with unsupervised, which she is still not permitted to touch. The permitted list grows slowly. She measures her progress by what she is allowed to touch alone.
> **Age 33 (700 TR)** — The merchant's wife commissioned a piece, was satisfied, commissioned another, and six years later is still commissioning work. This is what a successful practice looks like from the inside: not the exceptional commission but the reliable one, the client who trusts your judgment and whose household requirements are predictable and who pays on time. She has three clients like this. She is working on a fourth. She is aware that all of them are contingent on the merchant's wife continuing to find her work satisfactory, which means she pays attention to every piece she makes for this household as if it is the first one.
> **Age 34 (701 TR)** — She found it by accident. Old coin, mostly, wrapped in rotted cloth. More than she had expected. She told no one for a week.
> **Age 35 (702 TR)** — Crisis drew her back to her faith with new fervour.
> **Age 36 (703 TR)** — She had trusted this person. That trust had been a tool someone else picked up and used. Betrayed by Morin Dyer
> **Age 37 (704 TR)** — The gossip thinned. People forgot, or found newer griefs to occupy them. She was not forgiven so much as left alone.
> **Age 38 (705 TR)** — Necessity had long since made her skilled. She went beyond necessity.
> **Age 39 (706 TR)** — She bought cheap and sold dear, and the ledger proved it.
> **Age 40 (707 TR)** — She was sent to collect a pattern from the leatherworkers' guild house and waited while the guild officer found the document. She watched the cutting floor from the window. The leatherworkers had a different relationship with waste than her own trade — they cut for maximum material conservation, which produced a different kind of thinking about the pattern itself. She came back with the document and three observations she has been applying to her own practice.
> **Age 41 (708 TR)** — She fought for what was hers through every channel available to her.
> **Age 42 (709 TR)** — Another difficult year. Life continued.
> **Age 43 (710 TR)** — The other apprentice got the easier tasks, the better hours, the master's patience. She got the corrections and the night work and the jobs that were beneath notice if they went right and humiliating if they went wrong. She understood that this was partly preference and partly a test — the masters who treat their female apprentices harder are testing whether they will leave, because the easy ones always leave and the hard ones stay and become the best practitioners. She stayed. She is still deciding whether the theory justifies the practice.
> **Age 44 (711 TR)** — The franchise had come available when old Maret died with no heirs. She had moved faster than the men who assumed she would not, and had the backing of two syndics before the others had finished shaking their heads. The vote was closer than she liked. She did not let this show.
> **Age 45 (712 TR)** — A fighting order commissioning from a woman was irregular enough that the arms-master who arrived had clearly been told to pretend he did not find it so. She found this easier to work with than direct hostility would have been. The commission was for mail, which is what she does best, and when they came to inspect it, the pretense of indifference became straightforwardly professional. They have sent three subsequent commissions.
> **Age 46 (713 TR)** — Her childbearing years had passed. She felt the change in herself, and adapted.
> **Age 47 (714 TR)** — An incident in public damaged her name in the community.
> **Age 48 (715 TR)** — He endured. The details were different; the shape was the same.
> **Age 49 (716 TR)** — She attends the mixed evenings at the guild hall, which are fewer and more formal than the men's evenings, and she has learned to extract what she needs from a different kind of conversation. The women who come are mostly established practitioners who have figured out which parts of the guild structure serve them and which do not. The conversations are more direct. The information is more specific. She gets more from two hours of this than from a full meeting of the general guild council.
