> [!background] Background
> **Age 18 (688 TR)** — He made a habit of it — the early mornings, the regular exertion. It accumulated.
> **Age 19 (689 TR)** — His lord no longer needed him, or no longer wanted him. He was paid off and told to go.
> **Age 20 (690 TR)** — The knife came out of the dark on the Kolisa bridge. He was lucky — the cobblestones were wet and he slipped at the wrong moment, which turned out to be the right moment. He did not see the face. He lay in the mud listening to footsteps retreating and understood that someone had spent money to end his life and received inadequate value.
> **Age 22 (692 TR)** — He was posted to the garrison watching the Osel crossing. The first month he did not understand why everyone kept one hand near a weapon at all times. By the third month he understood.
> **Age 23 (693 TR)** — He stood in the crowd and watched a man hang. It was meant to be a warning; he was not sure what lesson he took from it.
> **Age 24 (694 TR)** — He watched a true expert do something he had done himself for years. He understood the difference immediately.
> **Age 25 (695 TR)** — The shout went up from the market quarter and he was close enough that joining was not optional — the custom is that any man who hears it and does not join is liable to be treated as an accomplice. He ran with the others for three streets before they cornered the man in a yard. The man had stolen a purse. He held one arm while the officers bound him. The whole thing lasted perhaps ten minutes. He thought about it afterward: the specific way the crowd becomes a legal instrument when the right words are shouted, and how different it felt to be part of that instrument than he had expected it to feel.
> **Age 26 (696 TR)** — Shamed by his helplessness, he paid for lessons with a man-at-arms.
> **Age 27 (697 TR)** — He had the broadsword because he found it, which is not a defence. He carried it for two months because he needed a sword and it was a good sword and he told himself no one would notice. Someone noticed. He was brought before the hundred moot on a charge of breach of rank: carrying chivalric arms without the legal right to do so. The moot fined him, confiscated the sword, and issued a formal warning that a repeat offence would carry corporal punishment. He paid the fine. He has carried a shortsword since, which is his right, and which he is careful to carry visibly so that there is no question about what it is.
> **Age 28 (698 TR)** — He took a wife, binding two households together. Married Aleth Warman (peasant, age 28)
> **Age 29 (699 TR)** — Campaign rations or lean winters had made him a competent cook.
> **Age 29 (699 TR)** — His wife found something he had kept from before they met — a letter, a token, an old drawing — and asked about it. He told her the truth. It was not interesting. She had expected it to be interesting.
> **Age 30 (700 TR)** — The Matakea walked the line in the hour before dawn, touching each soldier's shoulder. He had heard the Alamirata a hundred times in temple. Here it meant something different — the same words, the same cadence, but spoken over men who might be dead by midday. He had not expected to find it comforting. He found it comforting.
> **Age 30 (700 TR)** — He and his family took the wrong road on a journey together and were lost for half a day. Everyone had a different idea of where they had gone wrong.
> **Age 31 (701 TR)** — The first real cold came and he realised summer was over. He stood in it for a moment before going inside.
> **Age 32 (702 TR)** — Drink, pride, or an old grudge turned into something harder. He gave as good as he got.
> **Age 32 (702 TR)** — Another difficult year. Life continued.
> **Age 33 (703 TR)** — He had about forty words of Pagaelin and a captive who knew what all forty of them were. The Pagaelin watched him assemble his questions with an expression that was not quite contempt — closer to professional assessment. He got nothing useful and the captive escaped two days later, which he found he could not entirely resent.
> **Age 33 (703 TR)** — His wife fell ill — not the brief fever of a week, but something that lingered and frightened. She recovered, mostly. He did not forget the months of it.
> **Age 34 (704 TR)** — A lord or town sergeant offered him steady pay and a bunk. He stopped moving.
> **Age 34 (704 TR)** — Aleth Warman died. They had been married 6 years. He had not thought enough about what that meant until it was over. Aleth Warman died (approx. age 34)
> **Age 35 (705 TR)** — He heard something outside in the night, got up to investigate, and found nothing. He went back to bed and lay awake for an hour.
> **Age 36 (706 TR)** — Not fluency — nothing close to it. Enough to tell from tone whether the next thing would be a toll demand or a spear throw. Enough to count. Enough to say no and mean it, or yes and be understood. The rest he learned to read from posture.
> **Age 37 (707 TR)** — He struck up an acquaintance that would prove valuable in years to come. Made contact: Yoric Lalekar (noble)
> **Age 37 (707 TR)** — His parent told a story he had heard dozens of times as if it were new. He listened as if he had never heard it.
> **Age 38 (708 TR)** — Another difficult year. Life continued.
> **Age 39 (709 TR)** — The letter arrived with no signature. It described, in precise and accurate detail, something he had done three years ago that he had believed no one knew. The terms were not unreasonable. That was almost the worst part — whoever had sent it understood exactly what the information was worth and had priced it accordingly.
> **Age 40 (710 TR)** — He recognised something in the young and invested time in it. Mentored Voran Halman
> **Age 41 (711 TR)** — He was taken. Ransom, eventually, freed him — but the months in captivity left a mark. A serious wound to the chest or torso. It healed, but left a permanent reduction in stamina (−1).
> **Age 42 (712 TR)** — The Argent Order knight identified the man as Navehan, which explained several things about the last two weeks. He killed him in the tunnel mouth with a hand-axe in less than thirty seconds. The killing felt different from others he had done. Not better, exactly — but more contained, more purposeful. He did not examine this feeling very closely.
> **Age 43 (713 TR)** — He was called to the hundred assize as a juror — yeoman tenure creates this obligation and he has known it was coming since he was granted the holding. Three days of cases: a land boundary dispute, two debt matters, a man accused of breaking into a mill. He listened to each case and the testimony of witnesses and asked the questions the court procedure allowed him to ask and formed judgments. The judgment in the mill case he is less certain of than the others. The man was probably guilty but the testimony was inconsistent. He voted with the majority. He is still thinking about the inconsistency.
> **Age 44 (714 TR)** — His lord had given an order he could not follow. Not would not — could not, in the sense that following it would have made him someone he did not want to be and could not have looked at in still water. He refused, formally, with witnesses. He was not punished. That surprised him more than the order had.
> **Age 45 (715 TR)** — The year passed much as the last had.
> **Age 46 (716 TR)** — A friend — soldier, neighbour, or kin — died badly. The loss left a mark. Lothar Maling died
> **Age 47 (717 TR)** — Three priests, four acolytes, six guardsmen. They were told about the feigned conversions afterward, by the sole survivor who had been away for supplies. He had memorised the number. It was the kind of number that explained an entire policy.
> **Age 48 (718 TR)** — The death of a relative brought unexpected property or coin into his hands.
> **Age 49 (719 TR)** — He endured. The details were different; the shape was the same.
> **Age 50 (720 TR)** — Steel found him. The wound healed, after a fashion.
