> **Age 18 (675 TR)** — She married. She had opinions about the match that were not the primary consideration in making it. Married Noral Shoemaker (artisan, age 22)
> **Age 19 (676 TR)** — She requested reassignment from the guild and they gave it to her, which means the guild agreed that the situation in the first workshop was untenable without either party having to say so directly. The new workshop is better — the master is fair, the work is good, the other journeymen are not the kind of men who make the work worse by being present. She has been here four months and is already producing better work than she was at the previous place. She had not realised how much the atmosphere had been costing her.
> **Age 19 (676 TR)** — She bore a child. Wela Shoemaker came into the world healthy. week 40 Wela Shoemaker born (female)
> **Age 20 (677 TR)** — The mistress cuffed her across the back of the head hard enough to make her ears ring. The reason was a miscounted measure that had ruined two yards of good cloth. She did not cry — she had learned by this point that crying produced worse outcomes than silence. She fixed the count, restarted the section, and did not make the same error again. She has thought about the incident since mostly in terms of what the mistress actually communicated: that the cloth mattered more than the learning, and that the learning was her problem to solve, not the mistress's problem to manage.
> **Age 20 (677 TR)** — He died in the way that men die on the heath — quickly, and with no time given for preparation. She has been continuing since then. Continuing is not the same as being well. It is the thing before being well, if being well eventually comes.
> **Age 21 (678 TR)** — She has remade this join four times. The fourth time it is correct by every technical standard she can apply. She is sitting with it and it is still not right and she cannot say why. This is the part of the craft that she cannot explain to anyone who doesn't practise it: the knowledge that something is wrong before she can name what is wrong. She names it eventually, always. She is waiting for the name.
> **Age 21 (678 TR)** — The birth was hard and left her weakened for months. But the child — Amara Shoemaker — lived. week 40 Amara Shoemaker born (female)
> **Age 22 (679 TR)** — She attends the mixed evenings at the guild hall, which are fewer and more formal than the men's evenings, and she has learned to extract what she needs from a different kind of conversation. The women who come are mostly established practitioners who have figured out which parts of the guild structure serve them and which do not. The conversations are more direct. The information is more specific. She gets more from two hours of this than from a full meeting of the general guild council.
> **Age 22 (679 TR)** — Something became funny in a way that built and built until everyone was helpless with it. She remembered it for the rest of her life without being able to say what exactly had been funny.
> **Age 23 (680 TR)** — The morning begins with whatever the master left unfinished, which is the rule at every workshop and the reason early mornings in every workshop smell the same. She has been doing this for four years. She knows the rhythm of the place — which tasks come in sequence, which the master trusts her with unsupervised, which she is still not permitted to touch. The permitted list grows slowly. She measures her progress by what she is allowed to touch alone.
> **Age 23 (680 TR)** — The birth took longer than expected. Ista Shoemaker lived. She spent the next three days learning who she now was. week 40 Ista Shoemaker born (female)

The fourth supplier she tried was cheaper and consistently substandard — she tested three batches before concluding the variance was not batch quality but supplier quality. She has a mental map of the supply chain for every material she uses, who she trusts at each point and for what, and what to do when a source fails. This is most of the craft, she has concluded: the material before it reaches your hands.

She understood that this was partly preference and partly a test — the masters who treat their female apprentices harder are testing whether they will leave, because the easy ones always leave and the hard ones stay and become the best practitioners.
